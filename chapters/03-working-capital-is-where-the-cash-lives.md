
# Chapter 3 — Working Capital Is Where the Cash Lives


There is a number that most people outside of treasury operations have never heard of, and it is the single most useful operational lens a CFO has. It is not revenue. It is not EBITDA. It is not the stock price or the debt-to-equity ratio. It is a number measured in days, and it tells you how long a dollar of cost spends inside the firm before it comes back as a dollar of revenue.

That number is the **cash conversion cycle**, and this chapter is about why it matters more than almost anything else in the first half of this book.

Before I explain the number, I want to tell you something about where cash actually comes from — because most introductions to corporate finance get this wrong by omission, and the omission causes real confusion later.

---

## The Capital Source Nobody Talks About

The standard capital structure debate — which we will arrive at in Chapters 7 and 8 — frames every financing decision as a choice between debt and equity. Borrow money or sell shares. Pay interest or dilute ownership. The whole apparatus of leverage ratios, WACC calculations, and Modigliani-Miller theorems is built around this framing.

The framing is not wrong. It is incomplete.

There is a third source of capital that sits outside the debt-versus-equity structure entirely. It does not require a conversation with a banker. It does not require board approval. It does not add interest expense or dilute existing shareholders. It is generated by running the operating cycle more efficiently than you ran it last year.

It is called working capital efficiency, and for a firm of any size, it can be worth hundreds of millions of dollars.

Here is the concrete version of the claim. Halverson Manufacturing has revenues of roughly $2 billion per year and cost of goods sold of roughly $1.4 billion per year [verify]. Its daily cost run rate is about $3.8 million per day. If the finance team can shorten the operating cycle by ten days — just ten days, in a cycle that currently runs 85 days — they free up roughly $38 million of cash. That cash is already inside the firm. They do not need to borrow it. They do not need to issue stock to raise it. They just need to manage the operating cycle slightly better.

Thirty-eight million dollars. That is most of the budget for the Plant 4 expansion Maya is analyzing. Generated internally, without external financing, by reducing the operating cycle by ten days out of eighty-five.

That is what I mean when I say the cash conversion cycle is the single most important number in this chapter.

## What Working Capital Actually Is

The textbook definition of working capital is: current assets minus current liabilities. This is technically correct and, for most operational purposes, unhelpful. It lumps together cash, accounts receivable, inventory, accounts payable, and accrued liabilities as if they were all the same kind of thing. They are not.

Here is the definition I want you to use instead:

$$\text{Operating working capital} = \text{Accounts Receivable} + \text{Inventory} - \text{Accounts Payable}$$

Each term in this equation represents cash in a specific state:

Accounts receivable is cash that has been promised but not yet received. Halverson has shipped goods to a customer. The customer owes money. The cash is in transit, legally committed but physically absent.

Inventory is cash that has already been spent but not yet converted into revenue. Halverson bought raw materials, paid its workers to process them, and is now holding finished goods waiting for a buyer. That cash is locked inside the goods sitting in the warehouse.

Accounts payable is cash that Halverson owes but has not yet paid. Suppliers have delivered materials and are waiting to be paid. For as long as they wait, Halverson retains the use of that cash.

Operating working capital is the net of these three. It is the dollar amount the firm has committed to keeping its operations running — cash that is tied up between the moment it goes out the door and the moment the corresponding revenue comes back in.

Notice what is excluded from this definition. Cash itself is not in operating working capital. Cash is the *result* of working capital decisions — it is what you are left with after the operating cycle completes. Short-term debt is also excluded. That is a financing decision, not an operating one.

This distinction matters practically. The headline working capital number — current assets minus current liabilities — can stay flat even as the operating cycle is deteriorating. A firm can be accumulating slow-moving inventory, watching its collections slow, and squeezing its suppliers harder, all at once, and the headline number might show nothing unusual. The CFO who watches only the headline is flying blind. The CFO who watches the components can see the problem three quarters before it shows up in the cash flow statement.

## The Cash Conversion Cycle

Now we have the pieces. The cash conversion cycle puts them together into a single number.

$$\text{CCC} = \text{DSO} + \text{DIO} - \text{DPO}$$

Three terms, each measured in days.

**DSO** — days sales outstanding — is how long it takes to collect from customers after a sale:

$$\text{DSO} = \frac{\text{Accounts Receivable}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 365$$

**DIO** — days inventory outstanding — is how long inventory sits before being sold:

$$\text{DIO} = \frac{\text{Inventory}}{\text{COGS}} \times 365$$

**DPO** — days payable outstanding — is how long Halverson takes to pay its suppliers:

$$\text{DPO} = \frac{\text{Accounts Payable}}{\text{COGS}} \times 365$$

The CCC answers one question with satisfying directness: how many days, on average, pass between when Halverson pays a dollar of cost and when it collects the corresponding dollar of revenue?

Let me work through it with Halverson's numbers [verify all figures].

Revenue is $2.0 billion. COGS is $1.4 billion. Accounts receivable is $330 million. Inventory is $230 million. Accounts payable is $135 million.

$$\text{DSO} = \frac{330}{2000} \times 365 \approx 60 \text{ days}$$

$$\text{DIO} = \frac{230}{1400} \times 365 \approx 60 \text{ days}$$

$$\text{DPO} = \frac{135}{1400} \times 365 \approx 35 \text{ days}$$

$$\text{CCC} = 60 + 60 - 35 = 85 \text{ days}$$

Eighty-five days. From the moment Halverson pays for materials or labor to the moment the corresponding cash comes back in from the customer, eighty-five days elapse on average.

Now multiply by the daily cost run rate: $1.4\text{B} / 365 \approx \$3.8\text{M/day}$.

$$\text{Capital tied up} = 85 \times \$3.8\text{M} \approx \$323\text{M}$$

Halverson has roughly $323 million continuously locked in the operating cycle. Not invested in a factory. Not sitting in a bank account. Locked in AR, inventory, and the gap between what suppliers are owed and what customers have paid.

That is larger than Halverson's total long-term debt [verify]. It is six times the size of the Plant 4 expansion. The single largest pool of capital in the firm is not the fixed assets or the financial investments — it is the operating cycle.

## The Arithmetic of Improvement

Here is where the chapter earns its strategic importance.

Every day you shorten the CCC frees roughly $3.8 million at Halverson's scale. The improvement can come from three places:

Collect from customers one day faster — DSO down by one day. The mechanism: tighter credit terms, faster invoice processing, automated follow-up on aging receivables, early-payment discount programs. The cash freed: $3.8 million.

Hold inventory one day less — DIO down by one day. The mechanism: better demand forecasting, smaller buffer stock, faster supplier lead times, more disciplined SKU rationalization. The cash freed: $3.8 million.

Pay suppliers one day later — DPO up by one day. The mechanism: renegotiated payment terms, procurement leverage, optimized payment timing. The cash freed: $3.8 million.

The arithmetic is identical across all three. The strategy is completely different.

Each lever has costs, and part of what makes working capital management genuinely difficult is that the costs are often hidden or deferred. Tighten credit terms aggressively and some customers move to a competitor who offers net-60. Cut inventory buffers and a demand spike causes stockouts that lose sales and damage customer relationships. Push vendor payment terms from 35 to 50 days without warning and the supplier either reprices the contract to recover their cost of capital or deprioritizes your orders when capacity is tight.

The skilled treasurer does not pretend these costs don't exist. She asks a precise comparative question: is the cost of this lever smaller than the cash it frees? And she models the answer before pulling the lever, not after.

## Why the Headline Number Lies

I said earlier that the headline working capital number — current assets minus current liabilities — can stay flat while the operating cycle deteriorates. Let me make that concrete, because it matters for how you read a balance sheet.

Suppose Halverson's DSO rises from 60 to 75 days. More customers are paying late. The AR balance grows by roughly $57 million. But suppose at the same time the cash balance falls by $57 million — maybe the firm paid a large vendor early to secure favorable pricing. Headline working capital: unchanged. Operating working capital: $57 million worse.

Or suppose DIO rises from 60 to 70 days because a product line is selling slower than expected, but the AP balance rises by the same amount because the firm has been slow to pay one large supplier. Headline working capital: unchanged. CCC: ten days worse, concentrated in slow-moving inventory and a single strained supplier relationship.

These are not contrived examples. They are the kinds of things that show up in quarterly earnings calls as "working capital headwinds" — management-speak for "we noticed this too late."

The CFO who looks only at the headline is looking at a symptom. The CFO who looks at DSO, DIO, and DPO separately is looking at the causes. And the useful question is always whether the cause is structural (the business model requires long collection cycles) or operational (something broke and nobody noticed yet).

## The Diagnostic Detail

The CCC gives you the number. The components give you the diagnosis. But even the components require interpretation, and the interpretation requires getting past the aggregate.

Take DSO. A 60-day DSO at Halverson can mean three completely different things.

It can mean Halverson sells to customers on standard net-60 payment terms, and customers pay on time. The DSO reflects the credit policy, not a collections problem.

It can mean Halverson sells on net-30 terms, but the collections process is broken and customers are routinely paying 30 days late. The DSO number is identical, but the underlying situation is completely different — it is an operational failure that the aggregate number masks.

Or it can mean Halverson is booking revenue aggressively at quarter end, recognizing sales before invoices have been sent or payment terms have been negotiated. In that case the DSO is an accounting problem, not a collections problem. The AR balance includes a component that is not actually owed yet in any practical sense.

The way you distinguish these is to look at the AR aging schedule: how much of the AR balance is 0–30 days old, 31–60 days old, 61–90 days old, and over 90 days old? A healthy AR book has most of the balance in the early buckets. A deteriorating AR book has a growing tail in the 60+ day buckets — customers who are paying late or not paying at all. The aging schedule is the diagnostic that the aggregate DSO number cannot provide.

The same three-way ambiguity exists for DIO. Inventory growth can be rational (ramping up for a major product launch), operational (slow-moving stock that should have been written down), or strategic (deliberately building buffer stock against supply chain risk). The aggregate DIO does not tell you which. You need to look at inventory by SKU, by age, by turnover rate.

And DPO. Paying vendors slowly because you have negotiating leverage and they accept it is good financial management. Paying slowly because cash is tight and you are stretching vendors to avoid drawing on the revolver is a warning sign. Paying slowly because the AP system is backlogged and invoices are sitting unprocessed is operational dysfunction. Again: same number, different situations.

This is the reason Tom, Halverson's treasurer, spends his mornings on the daily cash report and his mid-mornings on calls with the AR manager about delayed collections. He is not being granular for its own sake. He is maintaining the diagnostic resolution that the aggregate numbers cannot give him.

## The Organizational Reality

There is something that the CCC formula does not show and that I want to name directly, because it shapes how working capital improvement actually happens inside a firm.

The three components of the CCC are owned by three different parts of the organization.

DSO is owned jointly by the sales function (which sets payment terms as part of commercial negotiations) and the finance function (which runs collections). Shortening DSO often requires the sales team to accept tighter credit terms with customers who push back, which sales teams dislike. The CFO cannot shorten DSO by memo. She has to negotiate it with the commercial side of the business.

DIO is owned by operations and supply chain. Reducing inventory means accepting less buffer stock, which means accepting more exposure to demand variability and supplier unreliability. The operations team has very reasonable reasons for wanting larger buffers. The CFO has very reasonable reasons for wanting smaller ones. The right answer involves tradeoffs that neither function can resolve alone.

DPO is owned by procurement. Extending payable days means suppliers get paid later, which has costs for suppliers and can eventually have costs for Halverson in the form of higher prices or worse service. There is a real ethical dimension here — large firms pushing payment terms to 90 or 120 days onto small suppliers who cannot afford to wait is not merely bad strategy; it is a form of coercion that some jurisdictions now regulate [verify]. The CFO needs to manage DPO with awareness of both the financial leverage and the relational and ethical consequences of using it.

The CCC is a number. The work of improving it is organizational, not mathematical.

## Where This Connects Forward

Chapters 4 through 6 will treat capital budgeting as a question about allocating a given pool of capital — NPV, IRR, the mechanics of evaluating whether to build Plant 4. Those chapters implicitly assume the capital exists somewhere and needs to be allocated wisely.

This chapter is about making sure that assumption holds. Working capital efficiency is internal financing. It competes directly with debt and equity as a source of capital for the firm's investments. A CFO who shortens her firm's CCC by 30 days at Halverson's scale generates over $110 million of internal capital — three Plant 4s — without issuing a single bond or diluting a single share.

The implication is practical and it is the single most important takeaway from this chapter: **before recommending external financing, ask what internal financing is being left on the table.** Every basis point of interest on new debt is a cost. Every diluted share is a cost. Working capital improvement is not free — it has the organizational and relational costs I just described — but it is almost always cheaper than external financing, and it is almost always less fully exploited than it could be.

This becomes one of Maya's standing questions in every memo she writes for the rest of the book. It should be one of yours too.

## What Would Change My Mind

The chapter's claim is that working capital efficiency is a significant source of firm value — that firms with shorter CCCs are worth more than firms with longer ones, all else equal. The empirical evidence for this claim exists and is plausible [verify — Deloof 2003, Shin & Soenen 1998 are the canonical references], but it is industry-dependent and the direction of causality is not entirely clean. Firms that are doing well operationally tend to have both shorter CCCs and higher valuations; it is hard to isolate which is causing which.

If careful cross-sectional analysis showed no relationship between CCC improvement and firm value — if firms that aggressively shortened their operating cycles did not trade at higher multiples or generate better long-run returns — the chapter's emphasis on working capital as a strategic lever would need to be significantly revised. I think that evidence would be surprising given the mechanism, but I am not certain it is wrong.

---

## Still Puzzling

There is a line somewhere between "managing DPO aggressively" and "using size to coerce smaller suppliers," and I do not have a clean rule for where it falls.

At one extreme: a large firm renegotiating payment terms from net-30 to net-45 with a supplier who accepts the change because the business relationship is valuable. That seems like legitimate negotiation.

At the other extreme: a large firm unilaterally extending payment terms to net-120 for a small supplier with no alternative customers, knowing the supplier cannot absorb the cash flow impact. That is coercion in any practical sense, even if it is legal.

Between those extremes is a wide range of cases where the analysis of the CCC improvement is clean and the ethics are genuinely murky. The auditing standards do not capture the distinction. Neither does standard financial analysis. I find this unsatisfying, and I do not have a better answer than: exercise judgment, and be honest about whose cost you are externalizing when you extend payable days.
