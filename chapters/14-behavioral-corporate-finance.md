# Chapter 14 — Behavioral Corporate Finance

---

## The Hardest Assignment

Late autumn. The Cardinal acquisition closed in September. Plant 4 is six months into construction. Halverson's stock has outperformed its sector by 12% year-to-date. Everything is going well.

Diane asks Maya to write a memo on what could go wrong.

Not a perfunctory risk section. Not a bullet list of manageable concerns at the end of an otherwise upbeat document. A genuine dissenting analysis — one that takes seriously the possibility that the Cardinal deal will turn out worse than current indicators suggest, and that argues for that possibility with the same rigor Maya has brought to every other analysis in this book.

Maya finds the assignment harder than expected. The integration is tracking against plan. The synergies are materializing on schedule. The customer retention numbers are holding. Everything she looks at says the deal is working. Writing a memo that argues otherwise feels like manufacturing a problem that doesn't exist.

This is the exact feeling that should put her on guard.

The difficulty is not that Maya lacks information. The difficulty is that her mind — like every analyst's mind, like Diane's mind, like the board's mind — is running a set of cognitive patterns that were useful in most situations Maya has ever been in and are specifically dangerous in this one. The patterns don't feel like bias. They feel like good judgment. That's what makes them hard to see and harder to correct.

This chapter is about four of those patterns, where they show up in CFO work, and what counter-pressure looks like.

## Two Kinds of Behavioral Finance

Before going further, I need to distinguish two things that both get called behavioral finance.

The first kind studies how investors behave in capital markets — overreaction to earnings news, underreaction to new information, momentum effects, the value premium. This is mostly Book 1 territory. It tells us something important about how security prices behave, but its implications for the person inside the firm making capital allocation decisions are indirect.

The second kind studies how managers, boards, and analysts behave when making corporate decisions. The same human cognitive machinery, different context: applied to the people who decide whether to build Plant 4, whether to acquire Cardinal, what discount rate to use, when to abandon a failing project. This is the chapter's subject. The distinction matters because the two kinds have different implications. Market behavioral finance says "prices are wrong in systematic ways." Corporate behavioral finance says "the people setting capital allocation are making systematic errors." Those require different responses.

The four patterns below are in the second category. They are documented in the empirical literature on corporate decision-making. They are not exotic or rare. They are the default.

---

## Overconfidence

The first pattern, and in some sense the one that sets up all the others, is overconfidence.

Managers systematically overestimate the accuracy of their own forecasts. This is not a claim about arrogance or ego. It's a claim about calibration: when a manager says "we expect $20M in synergies," the actual distribution of outcomes is wider, and more skewed toward the downside, than the manager's implicit confidence in that number reflects. The manager believes the $20M is a realistic central estimate. What the historical record of similar estimates shows is that it's an optimistic central estimate.

The empirical pattern is consistent. Capital project cash flow projections, on average, overestimate realized performance. Synergy estimates in M&A overestimate realized synergies, especially revenue synergies. Time and cost estimates for integration programs, system implementations, and major construction projects run systematically short. The base case presented to the board is, in the population of base cases, considerably closer to the optimistic end of the true distribution than to the median outcome.

Why? Partly because the analysts who build these projections are genuinely trying to be accurate and believe they are. Partly because the organizational incentives around projections reward optimism — deals that look good get approved, projects that look profitable get funded. Partly because the inside view of a specific project, with its specific team and specific plan, makes it feel different from the historical average of comparable projects.

Daniel Kahneman and Dan Lovallo called this the planning fallacy: the tendency to predict outcomes that are too close to best-case scenarios. Their proposed correction is reference class forecasting — deliberately setting aside the inside view and asking instead: how have comparable projects actually performed? The reference class is more pessimistic than the project team's estimate. It is also more accurate.

For Maya's Cardinal memo, the reference class question is: in integrations of similarly-sized industrial acquisitions, how do synergy realizations in years two and three compare to year-one tracking? The literature is not kind here. Year-one synergies are the easiest to capture — cost consolidations, procurement savings, headcount reductions. Year-two and year-three synergies, especially revenue synergies that depend on cross-selling, customer introductions, and combined capabilities, materialize more slowly and at lower rates than initial projections. This is the prior the memo should be anchored to, not the current six-month tracking.

## Anchoring

The second pattern is anchoring: the tendency for initial numbers to exert disproportionate influence on subsequent estimates, even when the initial numbers are arbitrary or outdated.

The original demonstration, from Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman in the early 1970s, was almost absurd in its starkness: subjects who spun a rigged wheel before estimating a quantity gave systematically different estimates depending on which number the wheel landed on, even though the wheel had nothing to do with the quantity in question. Random numbers shifted subsequent judgments in predictable directions.

In CFO work, anchoring is not caused by random numbers — it's caused by previous numbers that may have been reasonable at the time but have been updated for the wrong reasons ever since. The first revenue projection in a planning cycle becomes the reference point for all revisions, even when the assumptions underneath have changed substantially. Acquisition valuations anchor on the seller's asking price or the initial offer rather than on an independent analysis of intrinsic value. Sensitivity analyses run ±20% around the base case not because ±20% is empirically meaningful but because that's what the template uses.

The most consequential anchor in CFO work is often the discount rate. At Halverson, the 8% WACC that Priya computed last quarter is the number that appears in every subsequent analysis. It arrived via spreadsheet update from the prior quarter, which arrived from the quarter before that. The original derivation was careful. Whether it remains accurate given changes in Halverson's capital structure, credit market conditions, and equity beta over the intervening period is a question that doesn't get asked as long as the spreadsheet is working.

The counter-move is deliberately uncomfortable: rebuild the number from underlying assumptions before using it in any high-stakes recommendation. Not because the original number is likely to be dramatically wrong, but because the exercise forces contact with the current inputs and makes the estimate defensible rather than inherited. A number derived by reasoning is different from a number accepted by convention, even when the two are identical.

For Maya's memo, the anchoring risk is specific: Cardinal's integration plan was built on a six-month track record. Updating from "tracking to plan" to "tracking to what we expected at close" is not the same as asking whether the plan itself was set at the right level.

## Escalation of Commitment

The third pattern is the one with the most financial damage per instance: escalation of commitment, the tendency to continue investing in a course of action because of what has already been spent rather than because of what the future holds.

The logic of escalation is easy to state and hard to resist. The firm has spent $30M on Cardinal integration. The integration is running into headwinds. Pulling back now means the $30M generated nothing. Spending another $15M might salvage the situation. The $30M is sunk — it's gone regardless of what the firm does next — but it feels like evidence that abandonment is wasteful. The rational analysis says: ignore the $30M and evaluate only the incremental $15M against the incremental expected benefit. The emotional analysis says: we already have too much invested to quit.

In corporate settings, escalation is amplified by two additional forces. The first is reputation: the people who championed the original decision are still in their roles, and acknowledging the decision was a mistake has career consequences. The second is identification: the project has a sponsor, the acquisition has a deal champion, and that person's professional identity has merged with the project's success. Abandoning the project is not just a financial decision; it's a statement about the person who drove it.

The empirical record on acquisition performance is partly a record of escalation. Acquirers that underperform their projections in years one and two tend to invest more in integration rather than write down the asset and cut losses. Additional investment is approved on the logic that the synergies are real but delayed. Some of the time this is correct. A consistent fraction of the time, the additional investment merely delays the eventual write-down.

The counter-move is to reset the analysis as if the project were new. Ignore the sunk cost. Ask: given everything we now know — the current integration status, the revised synergy estimates, the organizational capacity consumed — would we initiate this project today at the current implied price? If the honest answer is no, the correct question is not whether to continue but how to exit at minimum additional cost.

For Halverson, the escalation question is: at what point would the firm honestly conclude that the Cardinal synergies will not materialize, and what would the exit path look like? Without a pre-committed answer to this question, the firm is vulnerable to drifting through multi-year escalation, approving incremental integration spend indefinitely while describing underperformance as temporary. The answer should be in the integration plan — specific milestones, specific performance thresholds, specific decision triggers — before the situation arises.

## Confirmation Bias

The fourth pattern is confirmation bias: the tendency to seek information that supports existing beliefs and to discount or reframe information that challenges them.

Confirmation bias is particularly strong when the existing belief has been publicly committed. Once the CFO has presented to the board that Cardinal will generate $40M in synergies, the CFO's relationship to subsequent evidence shifts. Evidence that the synergies are materializing is confirmatory and gets amplified. Evidence that they are falling short is dissonant and gets reframed — "a timing issue," "a pipeline build-up before conversion," "expected given the integration phase." The reframes are not fabricated. They are often plausible. They are also generated by minds motivated to find them.

In CFO work, confirmation bias shows up in the structure of diligence and analysis rather than in overt misrepresentation. Sensitivity analyses test the variables most likely to support the recommendation rather than the variables most likely to challenge it. Comparable transactions are selected from the universe of deals that support the proposed valuation. Risk sections of memos lead with risks that are already mitigated and trail off into vaguer language about the risks that would require changing the recommendation. Diligence findings that complicate the thesis get labeled "manageable" without analysis of what managing them would cost.

Gary Klein's pre-mortem technique is the best-designed counter-move. Before finalizing a recommendation, write a memo set in a specific future: "It is 18 months from now. The Cardinal integration has clearly failed. What happened?" The constraint forces the generation of specific failure mechanisms rather than general risk acknowledgment. Failure scenarios generated under this constraint tend to be more specific, more credible, and more uncomfortable than the risk sections produced through normal diligence — because the pre-mortem is designed to make the analyst identify failure modes rather than dismiss them.

The pre-mortem is not a prediction that failure will occur. It is an exercise that forces the confirmation-biased mind to generate the arguments it would otherwise suppress.

## What Maya Writes

The pre-mortem structure is exactly what Diane asked for, and it gives Maya a framework for the Cardinal memo.

Five scenarios, each with a specific mechanism, a probability estimate, and an early indicator that would allow Halverson to recognize it before it becomes irreversible.

The first scenario: a major customer of Cardinal's consolidates with a competitor or moves production in-house within two years. Cardinal's revenue is concentrated — the top two customers represent roughly 40% of revenue (verify against Cardinal's financials). If either of them reduces their Cardinal relationship, the synergy case survives but on a smaller base, and the acquisition price implied a multiple that assumed the top-line was stable. Early indicator: customer-level revenue declining more than 10% in any single quarter; a lost bid exceeding $5M.

The second scenario: integration consumes management bandwidth such that Plant 4's ramp, Halverson's digital transformation initiative, and Cardinal's existing operations all underperform simultaneously. The risk is not that any single project fails — it's that three projects compete for the same finite organizational capacity. Early indicator: the operations VP requesting CFO-level prioritization decisions; missed milestones on more than one parallel initiative in the same quarter.

The third scenario: Cardinal's founder — whose customer relationships and institutional knowledge are load-bearing for the integration — departs before the 24-month transition period on schedule grounds or conflict. Early indicator: unplanned founder absences; board requests for a transition coordinator before the agreed timeline.

The fourth scenario: a macro event — recession, commodity shock, supply chain disruption — compresses Cardinal's margins below the modeled base case. The synergy case may remain intact in absolute terms, but the slower realization pushes the acquisition's financial justification further into the future. Early indicator: demand decline in Cardinal's primary end markets exceeding 15% quarter-over-quarter; input costs more than 25% above plan.

The fifth scenario: a material liability that diligence did not surface — a contract, a regulatory exposure, an environmental issue — emerges post-close. These are rare but not negligible; complex industrial companies carry contingent liabilities that standard diligence horizons don't always catch. Early indicator: legal department flagging documentation gaps; unusual creditor or regulator inquiries.

In aggregate, across these five scenarios, there is something approaching a 50-60% probability that at least one of them materializes within 24 months of close. None individually breaks the acquisition economics. Combinations could. The integration plan should include the early indicators as explicit monitoring triggers, with predetermined responses defined before the situation arises — so that when an indicator fires, the organizational response does not require a fresh decision under pressure.

This is what Diane asked for. It is the document that confirmation bias, operating unchecked, would not produce. Maya's discipline is to produce it anyway.

## The Verification Chain, Seen from Here

Looking back across the book's decisions from this vantage point: the verification discipline that ran through each chapter — the independent re-estimation, the confidence interval reporting, the sensitivity tables, the reference class checks — was not just about catching arithmetic errors. It was counter-pressure against these four patterns.

The gap between the operations team's $87M NPV and Maya's $42M for Plant 4 was mostly overconfidence corrected through conservative assumption-setting and scenario analysis. The anchoring of Halverson's WACC at 8% required deliberate re-derivation before it could be used confidently. The capital structure recommendation required resisting the escalation toward maximum debt financing that the tax shield argument, taken alone, would imply. The Cardinal diligence required explicit counter-pressure on the three risks that the deal champions were underweighting.

None of this made the analysis perfect. It made the analysis more honest about what it was: a set of defensible estimates with named uncertainties, where the uncertainties were generated by minds actively trying to find the failure modes rather than dismiss them.

That is the standard this chapter sets. Not perfect foresight. Calibrated honesty about the gap between what the analysis shows and what the future will hold.
