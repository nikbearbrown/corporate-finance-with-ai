
# Chapter 10 — Raising Capital: IPOs, Secondaries, and the Cost of Going to Market

Diane has a question for Maya that sounds simple: what would it cost Halverson to raise $400 million of new equity?

Maya's first answer is straightforward. She finds the cover of a recent comparable offering, reads the gross spread — the fee the investment bank charges for running the process — and it is 4% of proceeds. Four percent of $400 million is $16 million. That is the cost.

Diane shakes her head. "That's the visible part. The real cost is what you can't see in the fee schedule."

This chapter is about both parts, and why Diane is right that the visible part is not the big one.

---

Let me start by specifying what kind of transaction we are talking about, because the same cost structure applies in two distinct situations.

An *initial public offering* is when a private firm sells shares to the public for the first time. The firm hires investment banks to price the shares, market them to investors, and manage the distribution. The result is public market access, a tradeable currency for acquisitions, and liquidity for the founders and early investors who have been holding illiquid stakes. The IPO is a one-way door — once you are public, the reporting requirements, analyst scrutiny, and quarterly earnings pressure do not go away.

A *seasoned equity offering* is when a firm that is already public sells additional shares. The mechanics are similar but simpler: the price is observable, the investor base is established, and the information asymmetry between the firm and the market is smaller. Halverson is already public. The relevant case for Maya is the SEO. But the forces that drive costs in both transactions are the same, and the IPO version is more dramatic, so I will use it to establish the structure before returning to the SEO arithmetic.

---

The visible cost — the gross spread — is the difference between the price the underwriters pay the company for its shares and the price they sell to investors. The underwriters buy low and sell high, pocketing the spread as their fee for doing due diligence, building the order book, marketing the deal, and providing price stabilization in the weeks after the offering.

For US IPOs, the gross spread has been remarkably stable for decades at almost exactly 7% of proceeds. Chen and Ritter documented this in 2000 [verify], and it has remained stubbornly near that level since. This is strange. Seven percent is a large fee. The companies raising money are often sophisticated, advised by experienced lawyers and accountants. The banks competing for the business are supposed to, well, compete. And yet the fee stays at 7%.

The explanations for this puzzle are unsatisfying. If it were a cartel, regulators would have broken it up by now. If it were pure competition, the fee would have drifted down over decades of market development and electronic trading. The most plausible account is that the 7% is actually a bundle — not just distribution, but reputation, analyst coverage, the bank's willingness to defend the stock in the aftermarket, and the informal commitment to be a long-term banking relationship. You are not just buying a transaction; you are buying access to the bank's machinery. The price of that bundle has been sticky.

For SEOs, the gross spread is lower — typically 4 to 5% for established public firms — because the firm is already known, the investor base is already holding the stock, and the information asymmetry is much reduced. Halverson, with its existing equity float and analyst coverage, would probably face an SEO spread around 4%. On $400 million, that is $16 million. Real money. Not the major cost.

---

The invisible cost is underpricing.

Here is the mechanism. The company and its bankers set an offer price before the shares are sold. They build an *order book* — collecting indications of interest from institutional investors, gauging demand, adjusting the expected price range. On pricing day, they set the final offer price and allocate shares. The next day, the stock begins trading on the public exchange. Often the first-day closing price is substantially above the offer price. Sometimes dramatically so.

That gap — the difference between what the shares were sold at and what they immediately traded at — is underpricing. And from the company's perspective, underpricing is money left on the table. If Halverson's offering prices at $40 and closes at $46 on the first day, the 15% gap means the company sold shares worth $46 for $40. Every share sold in the offering cost the company $6 in forgone proceeds. On a 10-million-share offering, that is $60 million.

Underpricing does not appear on the income statement. It does not appear in the cash flow statement. It does not appear in the press release about the successful completion of the offering. It is invisible in the accounting, but it is real in the economics — it is a transfer of wealth from the selling company (and its existing shareholders) to the investors who received allocations at the offer price.

Why does it happen? The most rigorous explanation starts with the fact that investors differ in how much they know. Some investors — large institutions, sophisticated hedge funds — have done their homework on the company and have genuine views on its value. Other investors — retail buyers, smaller funds — are less informed. When the underwriters build the order book, they know that the informed investors will only buy if the price is attractive, and they will pass if they think the company is overvalued. The uninformed investors cannot distinguish between a good deal and a bad one. As a result, uninformed investors get allocated shares disproportionately when the informed investors pass — and informed investors disproportionately get allocated shares when the deal is attractive. An uninformed investor, reasoning about this dynamic, should expect to receive shares mainly when the informed investors wanted out. To participate at all given this "winner's curse," uninformed investors require that IPOs be systematically underpriced — that the offer price be low enough that buying without information is still expected to be positive.

The underwriters know all of this. They set the offer price to be low enough to clear the market, including the uninformed investors who need compensation for their informational disadvantage. The underpricing is not an accident or a failure of negotiation. It is the price of getting the deal done.

There is a second explanation, less elegant but probably operating alongside the first: underwriters have their own incentives. The institutions that receive allocations of underpriced shares are grateful, and gratitude in this market takes the form of future deal flow, advisory mandates, and trading commissions. The underwriter optimizes across its entire relationship with the institutional investor community, not just for the issuer on any single deal. Issuing firms would prefer less underpricing; underwriters have weaker incentives to minimize it than the firms they represent.

The empirical record: IPO underpricing in the US has averaged 15 to 20% over the long run [verify against Loughran and Ritter's most recent updates]. In hot markets and technology sectors, it has run higher — the dot-com era saw average first-day returns above 60%, meaning companies raised less than two-thirds of what they could have raised if priced correctly. For SEOs, underpricing is much smaller — typically 1 to 3% [verify] — because the company is already public, the stock price is observable in real time, and the information asymmetry that generates the winner's curse is largely resolved.

For Halverson's $400 million SEO, assume 2% underpricing. That is $8 million transferred from Halverson's existing shareholders to the investors who buy in the offering. Combined with the $16 million gross spread, the total direct cost of the offering is $24 million — 6% of proceeds.

---

There is a third cost, and it operates differently from the first two.

When a public company announces a new equity offering, the stock price typically falls. The announcement effect for US SEOs has averaged negative 2 to 3% [verify, Asquith and Mullins and subsequent studies]. For Halverson with a $5 billion market capitalization [verify], a 2.5% drop on announcement is $125 million of market capitalization destroyed in the hours after the press release.

Why does the market respond this way? The information economics here are the same as in Chapter 8, but worth revisiting. Management knows more about the firm than outside investors do. When management decides to issue equity, investors update their beliefs about why. The most pessimistic reading: management thinks the stock is overvalued and is taking advantage of mispricing to sell at inflated prices. Under this interpretation, issuing equity is a signal that insiders think the current price is too high. Rational investors should therefore mark the price down on announcement.

This logic — Myers and Majluf's pecking order [verify] — predicts that equity issuance always signals bad news, because if management had good news they would prefer to wait, let the stock price rise to reflect it, and then issue at the higher price. The announcement drop is the market updating on the signal.

The mitigation is communication. If the company announces the equity offering simultaneously with a compelling specific use of proceeds — a named acquisition target, a project with a clearly positive NPV, a strategic rationale the market can evaluate — investors can distinguish between "management thinks we're overvalued" and "management has found a use of funds that requires equity." The announcement effect compresses. It does not disappear — even good deals see a 1 to 2% drop — but the $125 million in Halverson's case could become $50 to $75 million with a well-communicated rationale.

This is why Diane's comment about the acquisition case matters. If Halverson is raising $400 million to fund an acquisition whose strategic logic is clear and whose price is fair, the announcement drop is smaller and the market's long-term rerating upward (as the deal delivers value) more than offsets the short-term cost. If Halverson is raising $400 million for vague "balance sheet and general corporate purposes," the market's response will be more negative, and it will probably be right to be more negative.

---

Now step back and look at the total picture for Halverson's $400 million offering.

The gross spread is $16 million — visible, certain, and relatively small. The underpricing is roughly $8 million — invisible in the accounting, transferred to new investors, real in the economics. The announcement effect is $125 million of market cap that evaporates in the hours after the announcement — not a deadweight loss, but a reflection of information and signal, and recoverable if the use of proceeds is sound.

The direct, unavoidable issuance cost is $24 million, or 6% of proceeds. This has to go into the deal's NPV. If Halverson is evaluating an acquisition that requires this equity to fund, the acquisition's expected value had better exceed its purchase price by at least $24 million, because that is the cost of the financing even before considering whether the deal itself is good.

Now compare this to debt. New 10-year bonds at Halverson's current yield of 5.2% would cost $20.8 million per year in interest before the tax shield. After tax at 24%, about $15.8 million annually. Over ten years, in present value, roughly $130 million of after-tax cash outflows. Adding $400 million of debt also pushes Halverson's debt-to-capital ratio from 30% toward 36% — still within a reasonable range, but consuming financial flexibility.

The comparison is not clean because the two instruments have different structures: debt has ongoing service payments that can become dangerous if cash flows disappoint, while equity's one-time issuance cost is sunk the moment the deal closes. The right framework for the comparison is the WACC calculation from Chapter 5 — does adding equity or debt at these prices move Halverson's overall cost of capital in a favorable direction, given the project it is funding?

But the intuition from the comparison is correct: debt is cheaper than equity, in direct cost terms, as long as the firm is not approaching the point where distress becomes a real concern. The pecking order — internal funds first, debt second, equity last — exists because the costs are ordered in exactly that way.

---

Which brings us to when equity is actually the right answer.

The first case is when the firm has already taken on as much debt as its trade-off optimum allows. Adding more debt would push interest coverage below comfortable levels, trigger covenant restrictions, or raise the probability of distress to the point where the distress costs outweigh the tax shield benefit. Equity is the only remaining option to fund the project without materially increasing the firm's risk.

The second case is when the equity is fairly priced or modestly overvalued. Issuing shares when the market is valuing them below their intrinsic worth destroys value — you are selling a $1 asset for 90 cents. Issuing when they are fairly priced is neutral. Issuing when they are overvalued creates value for long-term shareholders (who are being diluted at favorable rates). The management team that built the firm through years of compounding and is now issuing equity in a hot market where its stock has run ahead of fundamentals is making a good financing decision, whether or not it is comfortable saying so publicly.

The third case is when the project being financed carries equity-like risk that debt cannot absorb. A risky acquisition, an R&D investment with binary outcomes, an expansion into a new geography where cash flows are genuinely uncertain — these are situations where debt's fixed payment obligations are dangerous if the project underperforms. Equity's no-required-payment property has option value in high-uncertainty investments, even at a higher cost than debt.

For Halverson's M&A case: does adding $400 million of debt push it past the trade-off optimum? At the current 30% debt weight on a $5 billion capital structure, adding $400 million moves the ratio to roughly 36%. That is within the range of normal capital structures. Debt is feasible. Equity becomes the right answer if the deal is so strategically attractive that it is worth paying the additional issuance cost to preserve debt capacity for future opportunities — or if the announcement, timed with a clear strategic rationale for the acquisition, will signal quality rather than distress.

Maya's memo will have to make this case explicitly. The financing decision is not separate from the deal decision. They are the same decision.

---

There is a puzzle worth ending with, because Feynman's students always appreciated knowing where the mystery was.

The 7% gross spread on US IPOs has not moved in thirty years of market development, computing improvement, electronic trading, and regulatory change. Every other fee in capital markets has compressed. Equity commissions have fallen to fractions of a cent per share. Bond spreads have narrowed. Derivatives pricing has become faster and cheaper. And yet the fee for taking a company public in the United States has been approximately 7% for as long as anyone has measured it carefully.

I do not know why. The cartel explanation fails — antitrust scrutiny would have broken up explicit price-fixing. The pure competition explanation fails — it predicts price compression that has not occurred. The bundled-services explanation accounts for some stickiness but not the degree of it. The most honest thing I can say is that this is a feature of the IPO market that remains unexplained in a way that should bother anyone who thinks carefully about it.

What I can say is that the 7% is empirically robust, practically important, and the starting point for any honest cost analysis of taking a firm public. The SEO spread at 4 to 5% is lower, and the reasons for the difference — reduced information asymmetry, existing investor relationships, a known stock price — are well understood even if the absolute level of the IPO fee is not.

Maya's $24 million direct cost estimate is right for Halverson's SEO. The invisible cost is the story she tells Diane about the announcement effect and how it depends on the quality of the deal and the quality of the communication. That part is not in the fee schedule. It is the part that matters most.

---

*A note on what the chapter simplified.* The underpricing literature distinguishes between IPOs where the issuer is a firm with ongoing operations and direct listings and SPACs, which have different cost structures. Direct listings — where no new shares are sold and existing shareholders sell directly into the market — have lower explicit underwriting costs but forgo the price discovery and institutional marketing of a traditional IPO. The cost comparison between these structures depends on the firm's specific situation and the depth of its existing institutional following. The SEO arithmetic in this chapter also assumes a clean overnight offering; block trades and at-the-market equity programs have different cost profiles, generally with lower gross spreads but less certainty of execution at a given price.*
