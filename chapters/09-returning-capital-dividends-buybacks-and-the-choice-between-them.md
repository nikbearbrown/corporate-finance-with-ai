# Chapter 9 — Returning Capital: Dividends, Buybacks, and the Choice Between Them
---

## What the Cash Actually Is

Mid-summer. Plant 4 approved, debt issued, construction underway. Halverson generated $280M of free cash flow last year. Capex is funded. The acquisition pipeline is thin. After holding a reasonable operating buffer, there is roughly $180M sitting on the balance sheet that has nowhere productive to go.

The board's capital allocation committee meets in three weeks. The agenda item: what should we do with the cash?

Before answering that question, I want to reframe it, because the framing matters more than the mechanics here.

The $180M is not, in any meaningful sense, Halverson's money. It belongs to the shareholders. Halverson generated it by deploying the shareholders' capital in operations, and now that the operations have generated more cash than the business can usefully reinvest, the obligation is to return it. The question is not really "what should we do with the cash" — the question is "how should we give it back, and what does the method of giving it back say about the firm?"

That reframe changes the analysis. A CFO who thinks of excess cash as the firm's resource to deploy is going to make systematically different decisions than a CFO who thinks of it as the shareholders' money being held temporarily. The first CFO builds empires. The second one writes checks.

This chapter is about writing the right check in the right form, and why the form is not a detail.

## Why the Form Shouldn't Matter (and Why It Does)

In an MM world — frictionless, no taxes, symmetric information — the form of payout is irrelevant. A shareholder who wants income but receives nothing can sell shares to create "homemade dividends." A shareholder who doesn't want income but receives a dividend can use it to buy more shares. The investor can convert between forms at no cost, so the firm's choice of form has no effect on shareholder wealth.

This is the same kind of measuring stick as MM's capital structure irrelevance from Chapter 7. The result is correct in the frictionless world. The frictionless world is where you start the analysis, not where you end it. Every place the real world departs from the frictionless one is a place where the form of payout does matter.

Three departures are large enough to govern the Halverson decision.

**Taxes.** Dividends received by individual investors are taxed at either ordinary income rates or qualified dividend rates — in recent years, the top qualified rate has been 20%, plus a net investment income surcharge for high earners. Share repurchases return cash only to investors who choose to sell, who pay capital gains tax on their gain above cost basis. Long-term capital gains rates are comparable to qualified dividend rates at the top, but the basis offset means the effective rate on a buyback is often lower than on a dividend of equal dollar value — and investors who don't need cash now incur no tax event at all. For a shareholder base with substantial unrealized gains, buybacks are tax-preferred. For pension funds and endowments, which pay no tax, the preference reverses: dividends are equivalent to buybacks, and their simplicity may be preferred.

**Information.** Managers know things about the firm's future that the market doesn't. A dividend increase is not just cash — it's a statement. Management is saying, publicly, that they are confident the higher payout is sustainable. This is a costly signal: if management raises the dividend and then has to cut it, the damage is severe, so management only raises when they genuinely believe the new level is supportable. The signal is credible because backing away from it is expensive. Buybacks send a milder signal — management thinks the stock is undervalued — but the commitment is soft. An authorized buyback program can be paused or cancelled without the same consequences.

**Clientele effects.** Different investors want different things from their holdings. Pension funds and retirees running income portfolios prefer regular dividends — predictable, automatic, requiring no trading decision. Growth-oriented institutions prefer capital appreciation and would rather the firm retain or buy back than distribute. A firm that has paid a regular dividend for decades has self-selected into its shareholder base: its investors are there, in part, because they want the dividend. Changing the policy abruptly evicts them without warning — they will sell, often at depressed prices, and the selling itself moves the stock.

These three frictions are not exotic edge cases. They apply to almost every publicly traded firm's payout decision. They are the reason the choice between dividends and buybacks is substantive rather than cosmetic.

## What a Dividend Actually Is

Here is the thing about dividends that took me a while to see clearly.

A dividend is not primarily a cash transfer. A dividend is a commitment.

When Halverson's board declares a quarterly dividend of $0.40 per share and sends those checks, the cash matters. But the deeper content of the declaration is the implied promise: we will do this again next quarter, and the quarter after that, and we will raise it periodically as our earnings grow. The market prices that promise. The stock price includes not just this quarter's $0.40 but the present value of the entire expected future dividend stream.

John Lintner documented in 1956 — in one of the most carefully observed studies in corporate finance — that managers treat dividends in exactly this way. They set dividends based on their estimate of long-run sustainable earnings. They change dividends smoothly rather than all at once. They are far more reluctant to cut than to leave dividends flat through a bad year. The pattern is consistent across firms and decades: managers act as if the dividend is a commitment, not a residual payout of whatever cash is left.

The evidence on what happens when that commitment is broken is unambiguous. Firms that cut dividends typically see their stock prices fall sharply on the announcement — the magnitude depending on the severity of the cut and how much warning the market had. The cut itself may be financially rational — the cash is needed for something more valuable — but the market interprets it as management confessing that the future they had promised was not the future they believed in. The signal overwhelms the arithmetic.

This dynamic creates a trap for poorly designed dividend policy. A board that sets the dividend too high — based on last year's exceptional free cash flow rather than sustainable earnings capacity — has committed to a level it may not be able to maintain. The mistake is silent for several years, while the firm earns enough to cover the payments. When earnings deteriorate and the cut becomes unavoidable, the reckoning is public and severe.

The discipline this imposes: the dividend level should reflect the firm's *sustainable* cash-generating capacity — the amount it expects to be able to pay across the cycle, through recessions and demand softness and commodity downturns. Not peak cash flow. Sustainable cash flow.

For Halverson, the current $90M annual dividend — roughly 32% of last year's free cash flow — sits at the low end of the range for stable industrial firms, which typically run 30% to 50%. There is room to increase modestly. The question is not whether the arithmetic supports a higher number now, but whether it would support that number in a weaker operating environment three years from now.

## What a Buyback Actually Is

A buyback returns cash by purchasing the firm's own shares in the open market. The share count drops. The remaining shareholders own a larger fraction of the firm. Per-share metrics — earnings per share, dividend per share, book value per share — rise mechanically without any change in the underlying business.

Three things are true about buybacks that the standard presentation often flattens.

**Buybacks are genuinely flexible.** A board authorizes a dollar amount — Halverson's $200M authorization — and management decides the pace. Buybacks can be accelerated when the stock is cheap, slowed or paused when capital is needed elsewhere. This flexibility has real value for firms with cyclical cash flows, because it allows payout to match cash availability without the signaling cost of a dividend cut. The firm can return capital when it has excess and conserve it when it doesn't, without making or breaking a commitment.

**Buybacks are tax-efficient for most investors, but unevenly so.** Shareholders who don't want liquidity don't sell; they continue to hold a slightly larger fraction of the firm with no tax event. Shareholders who want cash sell, paying capital gains tax on their gain — often at a lower effective rate than the dividend tax, and always with the basis offset reducing the taxable amount. The aggregate tax burden on a given dollar amount returned through buybacks is generally lower than the same amount returned through dividends, for a typical US individual investor. For tax-exempt investors, the preference is weaker or reversed.

**Buybacks can be abused, and the abuse is common.** The flexibility that makes buybacks useful also makes them a mechanism for financial cosmetics. Earnings per share rises mechanically when share count falls, which creates an incentive for management to buy back stock specifically to hit EPS targets — regardless of whether the stock is attractively priced. A firm that buys back shares at a premium to intrinsic value is destroying wealth for remaining shareholders even as the EPS line improves. Worse, some buybacks are funded with debt issuance: the firm borrows to retire equity, exchanging future financial flexibility for current EPS optics. This is not capital return. It is leverage increase dressed as capital return.

The timing record on buybacks is revealing. Firms tend to buy back the most stock near market peaks — when prices are high and the stock is least likely to be undervalued — and the least stock near troughs, when the buyback would actually be value-creating. The pattern is roughly the inverse of what a value-maximizing approach would produce. The explanation is behavioral and organizational: when the stock is high, cash flows are usually strong and boards feel confident; when the stock is low, cash flows are usually weaker and caution dominates. The market cycle and the repurchase cycle move together in the wrong direction.

Halverson's $200M authorization is currently unexecuted. Whether to begin executing it, and at what pace, depends on whether the stock is attractively priced and whether the capital might be needed for higher-value purposes in the next twelve to eighteen months.

## The Three-Decision Framework

Payout policy decides three things, not one. I want to be precise about this because collapsing them together produces confused recommendations.

**How much.** What fraction of free cash flow gets returned versus retained for reinvestment, debt paydown, or strategic optionality? Setting the ratio too high creates dividend sustainability risk. Setting it too low accumulates cash that managers may deploy in value-destroying ways. Michael Jensen argued in 1986 that this is one of the central agency conflicts in public companies: managers with excess cash will find projects to spend it on, whether or not those projects earn the cost of capital. A high payout ratio is, among other things, a governance mechanism — it removes cash before it can be misallocated.

**In what form.** Dividends commit; buybacks flex. Dividends reach all shareholders automatically; buybacks selectively reach those who choose to sell. Dividends signal sustainable earnings confidence; buybacks signal undervaluation and tactical capital discipline. The form matches the investor base, the tax situation, and the content of the intended signal.

**In what pattern.** Regular quarterly dividends build a committed investor base and signal stable long-run earnings. Variable buybacks signal tactical capital discipline without a recurring obligation. Special dividends signal one-time excess without creating a future expectation. Each pattern attracts different shareholders and creates different interpretations of subsequent decisions.

These three decisions interact. A firm that sets a high payout ratio through buybacks retains more flexibility than one that sets the same ratio through dividends. A firm that pays a low dividend but executes aggressive buybacks sends a different signal than one that returns the same total cash entirely through dividends. Getting the combination right requires knowing your investor base, your earnings stability, and what you want the market to understand about your view of the future.

## What Maya Recommends

Maya's draft for the capital allocation committee is built on this framework. The core recommendation is to do both, in proportions that reflect the different properties of each.

On dividends: maintain the current $0.40 quarterly dividend for now. If Q3 cash flow performs in line with plan, signal a modest increase — to $0.42 or $0.43 per quarter — at the year-end board meeting. This keeps Halverson on a dividend-growth track without committing to an unsustainable level. The increase reflects genuine confidence in sustainable earnings, not the windfall of a single exceptional year.

On buybacks: begin executing the existing $200M authorization at a measured pace over the next twelve months, with discretion to accelerate if the stock falls materially below its recent trading range and to slow if capital needs emerge. The buyback returns cash with tax efficiency and flexibility. It is not a promise. If the macro environment deteriorates or an acquisition opportunity emerges, it can be paused without the signaling cost of a dividend cut.

On the remaining balance sheet cash: hold it. Not every dollar of excess cash needs to be returned immediately. Halverson operates in a capital-intensive industry. The ability to move quickly on an acquisition or a capacity investment when the cycle turns has value that doesn't show up in a payout ratio.

What Maya explicitly recommends against: a special dividend (signals nothing useful about future earnings capacity and doesn't build shareholder loyalty), a debt-funded buyback (exchanges financial flexibility for EPS cosmetics), and a dividend increase larger than the sustainable earnings base can support.

## The Signal Halverson Is Sending

The board will announce the dividend and the buyback execution on the same day. The analysts who follow Halverson will read both simultaneously.

A modest, sustained dividend increase says: we have reviewed our long-run earnings capacity and we are confident this level is maintainable through the cycle. This is a strong signal, made credible by its cost — if the promise turns out to be wrong, the eventual cut will be painful.

A disciplined buyback execution says: we have more cash than we can deploy internally at acceptable returns, and we are returning it in the most tax-efficient form available. We are not manufacturing EPS through financial engineering; we are executing a board-authorized program on a schedule that reflects our view of the stock's value.

Together, the signals say: Halverson is a well-managed firm with stable earnings, excess capital discipline, and a CFO who understands that the form of payout is not a formality.

Payout policy is not the residual decision after capital budgeting and capital structure. It is its own discipline, with its own frictions, its own signaling content, and its own commitments. The CFO chooses what the payout says about the firm. The market listens carefully.
